The Doctrine of Separability in International Arbitration

Author: Dr. Johnathan Doe, University of Law

Abstract: This article provides a comprehensive examination of the separability doctrine, a cornerstone of modern arbitration. It analyzes the doctrine's theoretical underpinnings, its application across various legal systems, and its implications for the enforcement of arbitral agreements. The analysis concludes that while the doctrine is robust, its interpretation in the context of pathological arbitration clauses remains a contentious issue.

The principle of separability, often referred to by the German term Kompetenz-Kompetenz in a related sense, posits that an arbitration clause within a contract is severable from the main contract itself. Consequently, the invalidity or non-existence of the main contract does not automatically invalidate the arbitration clause. Interim Measures in Aid of Arbitration

Author: Prof. Jane Smith, Institute for International Commerce Law

Abstract: This paper presents a comparative analysis of the legal frameworks governing interim measures in support of arbitration in three key jurisdictions: England & Wales, New York, and Singapore. It highlights the convergence of standards for granting such measures while also pointing out significant procedural divergences that can impact strategic decisions for litigants.

The efficacy of arbitration often depends on the availability of interim measures to preserve evidence, prevent the dissipation of assets, or maintain the status quo pending the final award.
